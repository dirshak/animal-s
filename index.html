Animals

Dive into the animals of the Amazon rainforest

Jaguar (Panthera onca)

Population: Fewer than 15,000 in the wild.

Lifespan: Up to 12-15 years in the wild.

Diet: Carnivore, primarily preying on deer and fish.

Dangers to Survival: Habitat loss, poaching.

The jaguar is an apex predator crucial for maintaining ecological balance in the Amazon rainforest. Its role as a top predator helps control populations of other species.

Giant Otter (Pteronura brasiliensis)

Population: Approximately 5,000 individuals.

Lifespan: Around 10 years in the wild.

Diet: Fish and crustaceans.

Dangers to Survival: Habitat destruction and illegal hunting.

The giant otter is known for its social behavior and plays an important role in its ecosystem by controlling fish populations.

Golden Lion Tamarin (Leontopithecus rosalia)

Population: About 3,700 individuals remain.

Lifespan: Up to 15 years in the wild.

Diet: Fruits, insects, and small vertebrates.

Dangers to Survival: Habitat fragmentation and illegal pet trade.

This small primate is crucial for forest regeneration as it disperses seeds while feeding on fruits.

Amazonian Manatee (Trichechus inunguis)

Population: Vulnerable status; exact numbers are unknown.

Lifespan: Up to 60 years in the wild.

Diet: Aquatic plants.

Dangers to Survival: Water pollution and habitat destruction.

The Amazonian manatee is a gentle herbivore that plays a vital role in maintaining healthy aquatic ecosystems by controlling aquatic plant growth.

Red-Faced Uakari (Cacajao calvus)

Population: Vulnerable status; population declining due to habitat loss.

Lifespan: Up to 25 years in the wild.

Diet: Fruits and seeds.

Dangers to Survival: Deforestation and hunting.

This monkey is recognized for its distinctive red face and plays an important role in seed dispersal within its habitat.
